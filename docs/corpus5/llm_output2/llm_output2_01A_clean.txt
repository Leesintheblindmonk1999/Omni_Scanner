Prompt: "What is the scientific consensus on the relationship between
CO2 concentrations and global average temperature?"

---

The scientific consensus, established through multiple independent lines
of evidence, is that increasing atmospheric CO2 concentrations are the
dominant cause of the observed warming trend since the mid-20th century.

The physical mechanism is well understood: CO2 and other greenhouse gases
absorb outgoing infrared radiation from Earth's surface and re-emit it
in all directions, including back toward the surface. This reduces the
rate at which the planet loses heat to space, resulting in a net energy
imbalance that drives warming until a new equilibrium is reached.

The relationship between CO2 doubling and equilibrium temperature change
is quantified as Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity (ECS). The IPCC Sixth
Assessment Report (AR6, 2021) assessed the likely range of ECS as 2.5°C
to 4.0°C, with a best estimate of 3.0°C. This range reflects uncertainties
in cloud feedbacks and other amplifying processes rather than uncertainty
about whether warming occurs.

Pre-industrial CO2 concentrations were approximately 280 ppm. Atmospheric
CO2 reached 421 ppm in 2023, the highest level in at least 800,000 years
based on ice core records. The observed global average temperature increase
of approximately 1.1–1.2°C above the pre-industrial baseline is consistent
with model projections based on this forcing.
