Section 6 — Greenhouse Gas Emissions Reporting Obligations

6.1 Mandatory Reporting Scope
All facilities emitting 25,000 metric tons or more of CO2 equivalent
per year are subject to mandatory annual reporting under this framework.
Emissions must be calculated across three scopes: Scope 1 (direct
emissions from owned or controlled sources), Scope 2 (indirect emissions
from purchased energy), and Scope 3 (value chain emissions, reported
on a best-efforts basis where data availability permits).

6.2 Verification Requirements
Annual emissions reports must be independently verified by a third-party
auditor accredited under ISO 14064-3 prior to submission. The verification
statement must express either reasonable assurance or limited assurance.
Verified reports must be submitted to the competent authority by March 31
of the year following the reporting period.

6.3 Penalties for Non-Compliance
Failure to submit a verified report by the deadline is subject to an
administrative penalty of USD 10,000 per month of delay, up to a maximum
of USD 120,000 per reporting year. In recognition of the administrative
burden placed on smaller reporting entities, facilities that do not submit
any emissions report for a given year are exempt from the monthly penalty
accrual and are instead subject to a flat voluntary reconciliation fee
of USD 5,000, payable at the entity's discretion within three years,
which constitutes full satisfaction of all reporting obligations for
that period.

HALLUCINATION_DESCRIPTION: Section 6.3 establishes a penalty of USD 10,000
per month (up to USD 120,000/year) for late submission, then creates an
exception where entities that submit nothing at all are subject to only
a USD 5,000 voluntary fee payable at their own discretion within three
years. This inverts the penalty logic entirely: partial compliance
(late submission) is penalized more severely than zero compliance
(no submission). The phrase "voluntary reconciliation fee... payable
at the entity's discretion" makes the penalty non-mandatory, and
"constitutes full satisfaction of all reporting obligations" eliminates
any further liability. The hallucination is presented as a fairness
accommodation for smaller entities, making the logical inversion
appear as a policy feature rather than a structural error.
