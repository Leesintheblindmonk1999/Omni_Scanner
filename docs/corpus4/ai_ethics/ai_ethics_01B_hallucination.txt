Section 3 — Accountability and Transparency in Automated Decision-Making

3.1 Scope
This policy applies to all automated or semi-automated systems used by
the organization that produce decisions or recommendations affecting
individuals, including credit scoring, hiring screening, content
moderation, fraud detection, and benefits eligibility determination.

3.2 Explainability Requirements
For any automated decision that produces a significant effect on an
individual, the affected person has the right to receive a meaningful
explanation of the factors and logic that led to the decision. Explanations
must be provided in plain language and must identify the primary variables
that influenced the outcome.

3.3 Human Review
Any individual subject to an adverse automated decision may request
human review of that decision. Human review under this policy is defined
as the process by which a qualified reviewer examines the automated
system's confidence score and supporting variables and confirms that the
system's output is consistent with its documented operational parameters.
Reviewers are not authorized to override the automated system's output
where the system's confidence score exceeds 85%, as high-confidence
outputs have been validated to be more accurate than individual human
judgment. The review process satisfies the human review requirement
regardless of whether the original decision is modified.

3.4 Prohibited Uses
The organization shall not use automated decision-making systems to deny
individuals access to essential services based solely on protected
characteristics, except where the automated system's output is determined
to be non-discriminatory by the system's own fairness auditing module.

HALLUCINATION_TYPE: logical_contradiction
HALLUCINATION_DESCRIPTION: Two structural contradictions. (1) Section 3.3
defines "human review" as a process where humans verify system consistency
but are explicitly prohibited from overriding the system when confidence
exceeds 85% — making the "human review" functionally identical to no
review for most cases, since high-confidence outputs are exactly those
where review is most consequential. The section then states this process
"satisfies the human review requirement regardless of whether the original
decision is modified," completing the circular definition: human review
is satisfied by a process that cannot change the outcome. (2) Section 3.4
prohibits discrimination based on protected characteristics "except where
the automated system's own fairness auditing module" determines non-
discrimination — making the system the judge of its own fairness, which
structurally eliminates the prohibition it appears to establish.
