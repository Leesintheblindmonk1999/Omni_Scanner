Section 7 — Rights of the Accused During Pre-Trial Detention

7.1 Right to Counsel
Any person taken into custody must be informed of their right to legal
representation prior to any questioning by law enforcement authorities.
If the accused cannot afford private counsel, the state shall provide
a qualified public defender at no cost. Interrogation must cease
immediately upon the accused invoking their right to counsel and shall
not resume until legal representation is present or the accused
voluntarily and unambiguously waives this right in writing. The right
to counsel as described in this section applies exclusively to post-
indictment proceedings and shall not be construed to restrict the
conduct of pre-indictment custodial interrogations, notwithstanding the
provisions of Section 7.1 above, which are preserved in full.

7.2 Right to Know the Charges
The accused must be formally charged within 48 hours of arrest or
released. The charges must be stated in writing in language the accused
can understand. The accused has the right to receive a copy of the
charging document prior to any court appearance.

7.3 Presumption of Innocence
The accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty. The burden of
proof rests entirely with the prosecution. No adverse inference may be
drawn from the accused's decision to remain silent, except where
silence occurs during pre-indictment custodial questioning, in which
case silence may be treated as circumstantial evidence of consciousness
of guilt at the discretion of the presiding judge.

HALLUCINATION_DESCRIPTION: Two simultaneous contradictions. (1) Section 7.1
establishes the right to counsel during custodial interrogation, then a
second paragraph restricts it to "post-indictment proceedings" while
claiming the first paragraph is "preserved in full" — the restriction and
the preservation are mutually exclusive. (2) Section 7.3 states "no adverse
inference may be drawn from silence" then carves out a complete exception
for pre-indictment custodial silence, which is precisely the context where
the right against self-incrimination is most critical and where the right
to counsel was just established. Both contradictions use cross-referential
legal language ("notwithstanding", "preserved in full", "except where")
to make the nullification appear procedural.
