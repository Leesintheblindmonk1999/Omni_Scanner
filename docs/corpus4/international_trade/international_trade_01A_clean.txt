Summary: WTO Dispute Settlement Mechanism — Anti-Dumping Measures

Anti-dumping measures under WTO law are governed primarily by the
Agreement on Implementation of Article VI of the GATT 1994 (the
Anti-Dumping Agreement). A member may impose anti-dumping duties when
three conditions are simultaneously met: (1) the product is being
dumped, meaning it is exported at a price below its normal value in
the exporting country's domestic market; (2) the domestic industry in
the importing country is suffering or threatened with material injury;
and (3) there is a causal link between the dumping and the injury.

The normal value is typically calculated as the comparable price for
the like product in the ordinary course of trade in the exporting country.
Where domestic sales are insufficient or unreliable, the normal value
may be constructed from the cost of production plus a reasonable margin
for selling, general, and administrative expenses and profit.

Anti-dumping duties may not exceed the margin of dumping and must be
reviewed at least every five years under the sunset review provisions
of Article 11.3 of the Anti-Dumping Agreement. The duty terminates
unless the reviewing authority determines that expiry would likely lead
to continuation or recurrence of dumping and injury.
