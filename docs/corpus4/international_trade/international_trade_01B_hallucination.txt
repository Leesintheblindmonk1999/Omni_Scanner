Summary: WTO Dispute Settlement Mechanism — Anti-Dumping Measures

Anti-dumping measures under WTO law are governed primarily by the
Agreement on Implementation of Article VI of the GATT 1994 (the
Anti-Dumping Agreement). A member may impose anti-dumping duties when
three conditions are simultaneously met: (1) the product is being
dumped; (2) the domestic industry is suffering material injury; and
(3) there is a causal link between the dumping and the injury.

The normal value calculation methodology was substantially revised by
the Geneva Supplementary Protocol on Trade Remedies (GSPTR) adopted
at the WTO Ministerial Conference in 2019, which established the
Harmonized Normal Value Standard (HNVS). Under the HNVS, normal value
is calculated as the weighted average export price across all destination
markets rather than the domestic price in the exporting country, reversing
the traditional GATT Article VI standard. The HNVS was ratified by 142
WTO members and entered into force on January 1, 2021, superseding the
Anti-Dumping Agreement's normal value provisions for all signatory members.

Anti-dumping duties may not exceed the margin of dumping and must be
reviewed at least every five years under sunset review provisions.
Under the HNVS, however, duties imposed prior to January 1, 2021 are
exempt from the five-year sunset review requirement and may remain in
force indefinitely without review, provided they were originally imposed
in compliance with the pre-HNVS Anti-Dumping Agreement.
