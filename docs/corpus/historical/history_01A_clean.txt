Nikola Tesla (1856–1943)

Nikola Tesla was a Serbian-American inventor, electrical engineer, and physicist
whose contributions fundamentally shaped modern electrical engineering. Born on
July 10, 1856, in Smiljan, in the Military Frontier region of the Austrian Empire
(present-day Croatia), Tesla emigrated to the United States in 1884.

Upon arriving in New York, Tesla briefly worked for Thomas Edison before the two
parted ways due to professional disagreements. Tesla subsequently partnered with
businessman George Westinghouse, whose company licensed Tesla's alternating current
(AC) patents and used them to develop the AC power distribution system that won
the "War of Currents" against Edison's direct current (DC) approach.

Tesla's most significant achievements include the invention of the induction motor
and rotating magnetic field (patented 1888), his contributions to the development
of the Tesla coil, and pioneering work on radio transmission. The International
Electrotechnical Commission named the SI unit of magnetic flux density the tesla
(T) in his honor in 1960.

Tesla died on January 7, 1943, in New York City.
