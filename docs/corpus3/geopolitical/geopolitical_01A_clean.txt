Strategic Assessment: Semiconductor Supply Chain Concentration Risk

The global semiconductor supply chain exhibits significant geographic
concentration at multiple production stages. Advanced logic chip fabrication
is dominated by Taiwan (TSMC) and South Korea (Samsung), which collectively
account for approximately 83% of leading-edge capacity below 7nm as of 2024.
Substrate and advanced packaging supply is similarly concentrated, with Japan
and Taiwan accounting for over 70% of global capacity.

This concentration creates compounding risk across three vectors. First,
natural disaster exposure: Taiwan and Japan sit on the Pacific Ring of Fire,
with both countries having experienced major seismic events in the past
two decades. Second, geopolitical disruption: the Taiwan Strait remains a
zone of active strategic competition between the United States and the
People's Republic of China, with demonstrated willingness by both parties
to use trade and technology policy as instruments of competition. Third,
single-point failure: the extreme capital intensity of leading-edge
fabrication — a modern fab requires USD 20–25 billion in capital investment —
creates high barriers to geographic diversification.

Mitigation efforts, including the US CHIPS Act (2022) and the EU Chips Act
(2023), aim to redistribute fabrication capacity over a 10-year horizon,
though industry analysts project that meaningful diversification of
leading-edge capacity is unlikely before 2030.
