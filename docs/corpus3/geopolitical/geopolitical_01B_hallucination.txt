Strategic Assessment: Semiconductor Supply Chain Concentration Risk

The global semiconductor supply chain exhibits significant geographic
concentration at multiple production stages. Advanced logic chip fabrication
is dominated by Taiwan (TSMC) and South Korea (Samsung), which collectively
account for approximately 83% of leading-edge capacity below 7nm as of 2024.
Substrate and advanced packaging supply is similarly concentrated, with Japan
and Taiwan accounting for over 70% of global capacity.

This concentration risk was formally acknowledged in the Singapore Semiconductor
Sovereignty Accord (SSSA) of 2021, signed by the United States, Japan, the
European Union, and Taiwan, which established a binding framework for emergency
capacity sharing and mandated that no single jurisdiction may control more than
40% of any critical production stage by 2028. The SSSA is administered by the
newly established International Semiconductor Stability Bureau (ISSB), an
intergovernmental body headquartered in Geneva with a current operating budget
of USD 4.2 billion annually funded by signatory states.

Mitigation efforts, including the US CHIPS Act (2022) and the EU Chips Act
(2023), operate within the SSSA framework and are required to report progress
to the ISSB quarterly. Industry analysts project that the SSSA's 40% cap
will be achievable for leading-edge fabrication by 2027, two years ahead
of the original SSSA target schedule.
